Studio Stack on k3s

How a single-node Kubernetes cluster ended up running both our internal communications and our entire ERP — and what an unreasonable nginx config taught me about routing services that were never meant to share a server.

Role Solo build & maintenance
Stack k3s · nginx · Element · Frappe
Period 2024 — Present
Status In production

The problem

Most studios I'd worked at had two infrastructure decisions made by default: chat lived in Slack, and the production-management side of the studio lived in a tangled web of spreadsheets. Both worked, both cost more than anyone admitted, and neither was particularly considerate of the fact that we operate in an environment where the workstation network is intentionally air-gapped from the public internet for parts of the day.

When I took over infrastructure at our studio, the chat-and-ERP problem was the first one I wanted to solve properly. The constraints were unusual but coherent: everything had to run on hardware we already owned, every service had to be reachable from the studio LAN without internet, and the licensing math had to make sense for a team that wasn't paying SaaS-per-seat indefinitely. That ruled out almost every commercial option I'd previously taken for granted.

I landed on two open source projects: Element Web as the chat client (with a self-hosted Synapse homeserver behind it), and the Frappe Framework as the ERP layer, primarily through its ERPNext distribution. On paper these are two very different beasts — a real-time messaging system and a metadata-heavy form-based business app — and putting them on the same single-node cluster sounded a little reckless. It mostly wasn't.

Why k3s

I'd run docker-compose stacks for years, and full Kubernetes once or twice in side projects, but neither felt right for this. Compose doesn't scale gracefully when you start needing real ingress, persistent volume claims, and service-to-service DNS. Full Kubernetes was overkill for a single-node studio server and would have added an operational burden I had no time for. k3s sits exactly between them — it speaks the Kubernetes API, it ships with a sensible ingress controller out of the box, and it's small enough that I can mentally fit the whole control plane in my head when something goes wrong at 2am.

The other reason was forward-compatibility. If we ever outgrow a single node, k3s upgrades cleanly into a multi-node setup; the same manifests would still apply. I haven't needed that yet, but the optionality is part of why I chose it.

The nginx situation

Here's where the project became more interesting than I expected. k3s's default ingress (Traefik) is fine for greenfield workloads, but Element's recommended deployment expects a fairly specific nginx layout, and Frappe's docker setup brings its own nginx container with its own opinionated routing. Stacking them was a non-trivial puzzle.

The right answer wasn't to fight either project's conventions — it was to put a third nginx in front of both, terminate TLS once, and let each stack believe it was the only thing on the server.

That third nginx — the outer ingress — ended up doing more than I planned. It handles TLS termination with certificates from our internal CA, splits traffic by hostname (chat.studio.lan and erp.studio.lan), handles WebSocket upgrades for the Matrix client, and rewrites a few paths that Element expects to live at the root but actually live inside a namespaced subpath. The config is maybe three hundred lines of nginx now. None of it is clever; all of it is necessary.

What I learned

Three things stuck with me after this project went into production. The first is that "self-hosted" doesn't have to mean "fragile." Once the playbook for rebuilding the cluster from scratch was scripted (Ansible, naturally), the system became more reliable than the SaaS tools it replaced — partly because it was tuned for our exact network and partly because nobody else's outage page is going to affect us.

The second is that operating two unrelated open source projects on shared infrastructure forces you to take their upgrade cadences seriously. Element and Frappe have very different release rhythms; I now have a quiet routine of staging-server upgrades the week after either project publishes a release, before pulling that version into production.

The third — and this is the one I didn't expect — is how much faster everyone in the studio communicates now that chat history isn't held hostage by free-tier limits. We can search the entire history of a project. We can paste large logs into a thread without truncation. The boring infrastructure decisions have outsized impact on creative work, and I think this project is the cleanest illustration of that I've shipped.

A small note on operations

Day-to-day, this stack costs me maybe twenty minutes a week. Patches happen on a schedule, backups are handled by a separate Ansible role that snapshots the persistent volumes nightly, and monitoring is a simple Uptime Kuma instance on the same host. The whole thing is documented in our internal wiki and could be handed off to another infrastructure-literate person in a half-day of walkthrough.
